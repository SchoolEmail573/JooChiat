Booth 1 · Food Tasting

Sweet Heritage: Nyonya Snacks

You've just tasted three treats — pineapple tarts, ondeh ondeh, and kueh lapis. How well were you paying attention to their stories?

1. Pineapple tarts are a must-have during festive seasons. In Hokkien, "pineapple" is ong lai — what does it sound like it means?

Ong lai (王梨/旺来) sounds like "prosperity comes" — which is why the tarts are exchanged during Chinese New Year as edible well-wishes.

2. Bite into an ondeh ondeh and it bursts with a molten centre. What's inside?

The pandan-flavoured glutinous rice ball hides liquid gula melaka — dark palm sugar — and is rolled in fresh grated coconut.

3. Kueh lapis means "layered cake". How is it traditionally eaten by many Peranakan families?

Peeling and savouring each colourful layer one at a time is half the fun — and a testament to the patience it takes to steam each layer separately.
Booth 2 · Clothing Display

Threads of Joo Chiat: Dress Across Cultures

Peranakan, Eurasian and Indian communities all left their mark on Joo Chiat. Read the display cards closely — these ones take a keen eye.

1. The Nyonya kebaya is traditionally fastened not with buttons, but with a set of three linked brooches. What are they called?

The kerongsang — often gold and set with diamonds or intan (rose-cut diamonds) — was both a fastener and a display of family wealth.

2. What is the sheer, intricately embroidered kebaya top typically paired with on the lower half?

The kebaya is worn with a hand-drawn or block-printed batik sarong, often imported historically from Java's north coast — floral motifs signalled Peranakan taste.

3. Joo Chiat and Katong were historically home to a strong Eurasian community. Their traditional dress reflected which influence?

Eurasians trace ancestry to European settlers — largely Portuguese, Dutch and British — who intermarried with Asian communities; Western-style dress, adapted to the tropics, became part of their identity.

4. The Chetti Melaka (Peranakan Indians) blend Tamil and local Malay-Chinese culture. Which garment would you most likely see on a Chetti Melaka woman at a ceremony?

Chetti Melaka women kept the Indian sari but paired it with jewellery and customs shared with their Peranakan neighbours — a true Joo Chiat blend of cultures.
Booth 3 · Beadwork Display

Tiny Treasures: Peranakan Beading

Thousands of glass beads, one steady hand. A quick one — you've got this!

1. The famous beaded slippers of the Peranakans are called…

Kasut manek literally means "beaded shoes" — manek are the tiny faceted glass beads, historically imported from Europe.

2. Why did young Nyonyas traditionally learn beading before marriage?

A bride's hand-beaded slippers and wedding pieces demonstrated her refinement — some dowries included beadwork she had made herself.

3. Which motifs appear most often in traditional Peranakan beadwork?

Nature motifs carry meaning: peonies for wealth, phoenixes for grace, butterflies for joy — stitched one bead at a time.
Booth 4 · Heritage Theatre

The Joo Chiat Story

You've watched the short film on Joo Chiat's past. Last stamp — make it count!

1. Joo Chiat is named after Chew Joo Chiat, a wealthy landowner in the early 1900s. What was he popularly known as?

Chew Joo Chiat bought up so much land in the area that he earned the nickname "King of Katong" — and he allowed roads to be built through his estate, opening the area up.

2. Before it became a residential and shophouse district, much of Joo Chiat–Katong was covered in…

The area was plantation land — coconut, mainly — and a seaside retreat for the wealthy before developing into the neighbourhood we know from the 1920s onward.

3. Joo Chiat holds a special status in Singapore's urban planning. What is it?

Its pre-war shophouses, Peranakan roots and multicultural mix earned it Identity Node status and the title of Singapore's first Heritage Town.

4. The colourful, ornate shophouses along Koon Seng Road are famous examples of which architectural style?

Their pastel facades combine Chinese motifs (phoenixes, peonies), Malay timber fretwork and European columns and tiles — often called Chinese Baroque or Rococo.
